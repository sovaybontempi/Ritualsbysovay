Knot Magic Under the Blue Moon: A Ritual for Six

May 31, 2026 · Cord magic · Full moon · Community ritual · Sagittarius

Tonight is the blue moon — the second full moon of May 2026, rising in Sagittarius at 4:45 AM Eastern. We are taking a circle of six outside to work a knot spell together, using a macramé square knot pattern across eight strands, and I wanted to write up what I know about cord magic before we go — the history, the reasoning, and how the working is structured — so that what we do tonight has a foundation under it.

Cord and knot magic is one of the oldest and most portable forms of spell work in the world. You do not need an altar. You do not need fire. You need a length of cord, focused intention, and the deliberate act of tying your will into physical form. Those are the only requirements, and they have been the only requirements for thousands of years.

The history of knot magic

The practice shows up in ancient Mesopotamian clay tablets, Egyptian magical papyri, Norse seidr, Celtic folk tradition, Hoodoo, and the living center of modern Wicca and Wiccan-adjacent craft. Sailors tied knots to summon favorable winds. Folk healers bound illness. Cunning folk crafted protective cords. Witches worked knots for love, protection, binding, and release.

It was feared as much as it was practiced. In the fourth century BCE, Plato stated in his Laws that anyone who causes harm through magical knots should be put to death. The early Church issued formal edicts against the practice of Ligatura — knotted cord magic. Rabbinic law forbids tying knots on the Sabbath. Mohammed is said to have nearly died at the hands of witches wielding a cord of knots. The phrase "tying the knot" itself descends from Roman pagan wedding ceremonies, where couples knotted cord while speaking their vows.

What all of that fear says, more clearly than any praise could, is that this practice was taken seriously. It was considered genuinely powerful. The humble knot was not a metaphor — it was the thing itself.

In modern Wicca, Gerald Gardner formalized cord magic as a central practice. The cingulum — the nine-foot ritual cord worn by initiated Wiccans — is both a working tool and a degree marker, its color indicating the practitioner's level of initiation. The witch's ladder, a knotted cord threaded with feathers or beads, is one of the oldest known forms of constructed spell work still in active practice.

"A knot holds power. A knot can bind, release, protect, attract, and transform. The cord is not the symbol of the intention — it is the intention made physical."

Working with beads

Beads in cord magic function as anchors and amplifiers. Each bead marks a node in the sequence, creates a tactile boundary between one intention and the next, and adds its own energetic weight to the working.

The mechanics are simple: thread the bead before or after its corresponding knot — consistency matters more than order. Hold it briefly as you position it, the same way you hold the intent in the knot itself. The bead does not replace the knot. It accompanies it.

Color carries meaning: blue or silver for lunar energy and clarity; gold for abundance and expansion; green for growth; black for release and protection; purple for vision — well-suited to tonight's Sagittarius moon. Crystal beads bring additional layers: amethyst for vision, clear quartz to amplify, labradorite for transformation, moonstone for lunar attunement. Wooden beads from significant trees — oak, rowan, hawthorn — bring plant spirit energy into the cord.

A beaded cord becomes an object with presence. It feels different in the hand than plain cord. That difference is the point.

The blue moon — what it means tonight

A blue moon is the second full moon in a single calendar month. They occur every two to three years, which is the origin of the phrase "once in a blue moon" — genuinely rare, genuinely worth marking.

May opened on May 1 with a full moon in Scorpio: introspective, shadow-facing, transformative energy. Tonight closes the month with fire. The arc of May has been a complete working in itself — go inward and excavate what is true, then come back out and say it. Scorpio asked for the excavation. Sagittarius demands the saying.

Blue moons carry the energy of amplification and completion. Traditional associations include second chances, the closing of long cycles, and the number 13 — linked to the divine feminine and to the thirteen full moons in a solar year. What is set in motion under a blue moon is considered to have unusual potency and longevity. This is not the moon for small, hedged wishes.

Tonight's sky

The moon peaks at 4:45 AM Eastern, which means it is at full power for our entire evening. It is in Sagittarius — the mutable fire sign, truth-seeker, expansive, blunt, impatient with stagnation. The sun is in Gemini, its direct opposition, which means the tension in tonight's sky is between detail and vision, between what is in front of us and what is possible. Sagittarius will win that argument tonight. Zoom out. See the larger shape of what you are building.

The moon is also sitting in a grand cross with the North and South Nodes of Destiny — two points in astrology connected to fate and life purpose. This is what astrologers mean when they say tonight feels fated. The themes that have been building over the past year are in the air. The moon is also making beautiful alignments with Saturn, Neptune, and Pluto, lending unusual depth, vision, and durability to what is intended tonight.

It is a significant sky. Work with it.

Preparation before the circle

Before you arrive, spend a few minutes with your intention. Not a list — one core thing. Sagittarius energy favors the clear, direct statement over the complex wish. What is the one thing you are ready to move toward? Write it down. You will need to say it in one sentence when it is your turn.

Consider also what you are releasing. The full moon is always completion as much as intention. If something has burned eternally in your life — if there is a fire you are finally ready to stop fighting — this cord can hold that too.

The working — square knots, eight strands

Each cord was built from eight strands. We alternated the square knot pattern between the two outer sets of four and the inner set of four, working down the length of the cord in that rotation. On the inner set, a bead went into the center of the knot sometimes — not every knot, just when it felt right to mark that one. If you've never tied a square knot before, this tutorial covers the basic pattern — that's what I taught the group before we started.

Before tightening each knot, we settled into a breath — inhale, then exhale. The knot itself was pulled tight on the exhale, and that exhale was where the intention went. Every single knot, tightened on an out-breath, carrying whatever we were putting into the cord in that moment. We were each working at our own pace, not in unison, but sitting together in the same circle doing the same thing.

After the working, hold the completed cords in the center briefly. Feel what was just built. Then the facilitator closes with gratitude and lets the circle breathe.

After tonight

Keep the cord somewhere you will see it — altar, doorknob, wrist. Some practitioners untie a knot as each intention manifests, releasing the energy consciously. When the working is complete, the cord can be burned, buried, or given to moving water. If any knot loosens or falls undone on its own, pay attention. The cord is communicating.

Note: This working was designed for outdoor use where open flame is impractical — no candles required. If your group includes anyone who finds breathwork or extended focused silence difficult, simply invite them to hold their cord and witness rather than tie. Presence is participation.
