The Fire Is a Trick

May 28, 2026 · Shamanic journey · Personal essay · Lower world

I was meditating this morning and didn't feel like I had enough time to do a full shamanic journey. I saw one of the holes in the ground leading to the lower worlds and thought I'd go tomorrow, when I had more space. Something pulled me and said: no. Today.

So I went.

The lower world is usually trees. Forest, mountains, something green and quiet. Today I dropped in and it was on fire. Everything burning. People running in every direction, screaming, panicked, carrying buckets. The air thick with smoke and noise.

Someone grabbed my arm in the dark. I couldn't see them clearly. They started pulling me through the streets at a run — past buildings on fire, past people crying and yelling, turning down one lane and then another, everything burning the same way. Then they pulled me through a door into a temple building that looked like it was on fire from the outside.

Inside was a different world entirely. Children playing on the floor. People resting, talking quietly, sitting in their own stillness. Someone drinking a cup of water. The door closed and the noise stopped.

What the guide said

The person who had pulled me there turned and said: they don't know the fire is a trick.

The people outside — screaming, exhausted, running with their buckets every single day — they believe the buildings are burning down. But the buildings never burn down. Every morning they wake up and the fires are still there, exactly as they were. The people collapse from exhaustion. They spend their whole lives fighting fires that cannot be put out and do not need to be put out. The fires are not real. They are a mechanism to keep everyone panicked, running, depleted. It is working.

The guide told me he had spent years fighting those same fires before he could see clearly, just for a moment, that it was a trick. He found the temple. At first he just rested — slept, ate, took up the spaciousness of having somewhere that was not burning. The food replenished itself. He could venture out when needed, get what he needed, and come back. He stayed there for a long time just recovering.

Eventually he got lonely. He started going out to pull other people in — to show them the temple, to show them the fire was a trick. It didn't work for everyone. Some people, even after they saw the inside of the temple, went back out. Their houses, they said. They couldn't leave their houses to burn.

But some stayed. Slowly they built something. He showed me groups of people getting ready to go out: these ones are going for food today. These ones are looking for other places that aren't on fire. These ones are going to try to bring someone else in.

"The buildings don't burn down. They just are eternally burning. The fire is a trick — and it is keeping all of us running."

What I woke up to

I came back from the journey and lay there taking stock of my morning. I had woken up anxious. My acid reflux was acting up before I even got out of bed, and my first thought was that I was failing at taking care of myself — that I hadn't been doing the right things, that I was behind, that there were too many things to catch up on. I had a headache. I was frustrated that I had a headache. Frustrated that the frustration was making it worse.

Running through burning streets with a bucket.

The fires in my own life are real enough to feel. The reflux, the fatigue, the work left undone, the gap between who I want to be and how I actually spent yesterday. I feel all of it as emergency. But most of these fires have been burning in some form for years. I have fought them for years. The buildings are still standing.

I am not sure I know yet how to stop running. But I think the lesson today was that the first step is simply to see it — to catch myself mid-sprint, bucket in hand, and recognize the pattern for what it is. Exhaustion dressed up as urgency. Panic that has forgotten it had a beginning.

What I want to carry forward

The temple exists. That is the part I want to hold onto. The guide didn't tell me to stop caring or to abandon everyone outside. He showed me that rest was possible, that food replenished, that there were people building something quieter alongside all the noise. The choice is not between fighting and abandoning. It is between fighting fires that cannot be extinguished and building something that can actually last.

Today I want to spend some time in the temple. Not fixing. Not catching up. Not fighting. Just building something more sustainable than the sprint.

✦   ✦   ✦

I don't know if this resolves into a lesson with clean edges. I am still tired. My head still hurts. But something shifted in the journey — enough that I wanted to write it down before the day could talk me out of it.

The fire is a trick. I want to remember that.
