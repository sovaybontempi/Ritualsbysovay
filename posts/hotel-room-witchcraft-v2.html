Hotel Room Witchcraft

July 15, 2026 · Offerings · Gratitude · Practice

I am on a work trip. No tarot cards, no altar tools, none of the things I usually reach for. I did not expect to need them tonight, but the need showed up anyway. I am, as it happens, in Salem — Salem, Virginia, one of the many Salems scattered around the world that are not the Salem people mean when they say the name.

What I was carrying without realizing it

Dinner with the team, then drinks with a coworker, talking through recent events at work. Somewhere in that conversation I understood, clearly and all at once, that two large workings I had done were successful. One of them was a banishment — someone causing harm in a situation I needed them out of, sent instead somewhere they could learn and grow. I had asked for that outcome and mostly forgotten I asked. It is easy to send a working out and lose track of it. This was the moment it came back into focus: it had actually happened, and it was not the likely outcome on its own.

I noticed something else in that same conversation. A new work problem came up, and I caught myself already reaching for the next working — what could I do about this one — before I had paused to acknowledge the one that had already landed. That is a habit worth naming. Moving straight from a completed working to the next request, without stopping to give thanks, is its own kind of forgetting.

"When the work is successful, appreciation is not optional. It is the thing that closes the loop."

Building an altar from what was in the room

Back in the hotel room, the pull to give thanks was immediate and specific. No incense, no candles, none of the usual materials. So the question became: what do I actually have?

  • A black shirt, laid flat — it happened to fall into the shape of a cross, and became the altar cloth
  • A soap dish from the bathroom, rinsed carefully and filled with fresh water — water is always enough when nothing else is available
  • Two black coffee cup lids, used as small dishes for two cookies and four black cherries
  • Perfume, sprayed over the altar cloth as an offering, then set down on the altar itself
  • Dragonfly exoskeletons, collected from a wall in the Poconos where I had watched dragonflies emerge and fly off, kept in a cold medication box in my purse — one placed on the altar as an offering
  • Ten cherry pits, cleaned with a paper towel and rinsed with soap and water, placed in the cup
  • Fallen eyelashes, added to the water — the wish you make on an eyelash, given back to the guides who helped it come true
  • A small green plastic sword, the skewer from a cocktail earlier in the week, worn that night as an earring through its looped handle — my athame for the occasion

The exoskeleton mattered more than anything else on that altar. It is the shed form of a creature that changed completely and moved on. I had been carrying a physical symbol of transformation around in my purse for weeks without planning to use it for anything. It turned out to be exactly what the moment needed — a marker of change, offered in thanks for two workings that had, in their own way, changed something too.

The cherry pits and the eyelashes came from the same place: nothing about them was purchased or prepared in advance. They were simply what the last few days had already handed me — proof that a hotel room with no tools is not actually empty. The plastic sword just made me laugh, and I let it stand in as the blade. An athame does not need to be forged to do its work.

✦   ✦   ✦

Opening the circle

I called in my guides, my helpers, my ancestors, my deities, and the elements, and asked them to witness the offerings and take what they wanted from them. I was clear about why I was there: only to give thanks and make offerings. Nothing to ask for tonight.

At some point I looked at the clock and it was 11:11. I watched it stay 11:11 for the whole time it takes to notice something like that, and I used the moment to peel off the wish that comes with it — not making the wish for myself, but drawing it out into a small ball of energy, then into an orb of glass. I packed the orb into a little gift box to carry with me into the spirit space, unsure yet who it was for.

Alongside the wish, I brought a bag of goodwill — small glowing hearts, each one a piece of love and appreciation to give to whatever spirit wanted one. I didn't go down into the underworld this time. I opened myself and stepped sideways into the spirit space instead.

The insects, and the guide who finally asked for something

My insect helpers came first. A number of them arrived and each took a little glowing heart. I gave them love and energy, and they wandered off, satisfied.

Then one of my main guides arrived — someone who has been with me for years, steady and supportive, who I will not name here. I asked what I could do for them, after everything they've done for me. They didn't answer in words. They turned and walked, and I followed.

They led me to a house I had never seen before. It was theirs. They wanted it painted — bright lime green, pink, a pearlescent sheer white, deep ocean blue, and a sunset orange. I sat down and, with love and energy, built them everlasting paint that would never run dry. Brushes, rollers, trays, and a magic eraser to undo any mistake. I loaded it all onto a trolley and handed it over.

This was the first time this guide has ever asked me for anything. They are always the one giving, always present, never asking. Being able to give them something back — something as ordinary and joyful as paint for a house — mattered more than almost anything else that happened that night. They went through the paints delighted, already picturing the walls.

The wish, and the child who wasn't given it directly

After that, my maternal ancestor came and sat beside me. She wanted the 11:11 wish — I hadn't yet found anyone to give it to. She asked me to follow her, and we walked under a waterfall, over sharp gravel, onto pavement, and down onto a basketball court. In the corner sat a child, alone.

She didn't hand the wish to the child directly. The child had a guardian spirit nearby, and giving the wish straight to the child wouldn't have let them use it fully yet. So she gave it to the guardian spirit instead, to hold and use when the time is right. We walked back together, and I closed the circle.

✦   ✦   ✦

Notes from practice

None of the altar required anything I had packed. A shirt, water, snacks, perfume, cherry pits, eyelashes, a plastic sword, and something collected on a walk weeks earlier — all of it was already there, and all of it was enough. An altar is not the objects. It is the attention.

The ritual itself was a good reminder. I don't do enough workings that are only offering — energy given without a request attached, gratitude that isn't tied to asking for the next thing. My guide asking for something, after years of only giving, was the clearest sign of what that kind of practice can open up.

Note: When a working succeeds, give thanks before moving to the next request. The instinct to immediately ask for the next thing is worth noticing and slowing down.
